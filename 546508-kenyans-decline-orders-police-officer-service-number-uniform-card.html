Kenyans Decline Orders from Police Officer Without Service Number on His Uniform: &quot;Your Card Please&quot;

Posted by Martina Birk on Sunday, June 9, 2024
  • The National Police Service clarified that uniformed police officers had the authority to enter, search and drive a civilian's vehicles in permissible circumstances
  • The Kenyan law also indicates that police officers must properly identify themselves before entering and searching one's vehicle and document everything they take
  • Aggrieved motorists accused the police officer of attempting to search their vehicle and demand passports, yet he was unidentifiable

Michael Ollinga is a journalist at TUKO.co.ke with over 10 years of experience covering courts and crimes, special reports, and current affairs in Kenya.

There was drama after two motorists resisted orders from a police officer who allegedly entered their motor vehicle and demanded they produce their passports.

Motorists resist orders from a police officer

In a video that has ignited varied opinions about the conduct of the motorists and the police officers, the enraged civilians are heard asking the police officer to identify himself first and let them know why he entered their car.

"Why do you want to arrest us? What have we done? You cannot just stop our car and enter inside without telling us why you are arresting us. You are doing wrong. This is how corruption begins," one of the aggrieved citizens is heard saying.

On the other hand, one of the officers in the old Kenya Police uniform is persistent and demands cooperation from the arguing civilians.

Should Kenya police uniforms have service numbers?

The officer wonders why the citizens would demand that he present his job identification card, yet he is in uniform, and his orders must be respected.

"You cannot threaten me. You don’t recognise police officers in uniform," the officer responds.

The motorists, however, reiterated that the officer had not properly identified himself and given them a reason for asking for their passports.

"If you are a police officer, show us your work card. What is your name, then? And let us know what wrong we have done?" they posed.

The officer, under pressure, only identified himself as Michael.

Kenyans weighed in on the confrontation, some accusing the civilians of disrespect, while others said the police officer was wrong because he did not display his service number.

Waithira Munyiri argued that the manner in which the aggrieved parties addressed the officers was indicative of their misconduct.

"Aggrieved as you may be but intimidation, abuse of office, that inane phrase “Kenyans” love to use “do you know who I am” should stop…it’s precisely because of everyone feeling they’re more entitled than the next person that we are where we are," said Munyiri.

Tom Okinyi questioned why the police officer's uniform had no service number.

Can police officers enter and search your car?

"There is no police number on his shirt. Is it being concealed on account of mischief? Then the "victims" should also calm down in this engagement," he said.

"That officer removed his number quite interestingly, meaning he knows whatever he is doing is wrong!"Jully posed.

Another Kenyan said that the police officer had failed to identify himself.

"The 'police officer' is unidentifiable. He has no service number, and he can't produce his certificate of employment," Joseph Mus said.

TUKO.co.ke had earlier reported about the legality of uniformed police officers entering citizens' vehicles.

The National Police Service said that uniformed police officers had the authority to enter and search a motorist's vehicle.

"It shall be lawful for any police officer in uniform to stop any vehicle, and for any police officer, licensing officer, or inspector—(a) to enter any vehicle; (b) to drive any vehicle or cause any vehicle to be driven; (c) upon reasonable suspicion of any offence under this Act, to order and require the owner of any vehicle to bring the vehicle to him," stated the service.
